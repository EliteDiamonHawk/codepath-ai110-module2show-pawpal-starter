## 1. System Design

**a. Initial design**

- Briefly describe your initial UML design.
- What classes did you include, and what responsibilities did you assign to each?

I created a uml design with five classes. There is Owner, Schedule, Pet, Task, and Scheduler. I made the scheduler the unifier of everything as I needed a way to connect Schedule with the rest of the classes. Owner, will own Pet(s). Pet will own Tasks, since tasks is assigned to each Pet. Scheduler will then create a Scheduler wil then create a immutable Schulee object that displays information about said schedule. the Uml generated by Claude and reviewed by me is in uml.mmd

**b. Design changes**
- Did your design change during implementation?
- If yes, describe at least one change and why you made it.

I decided to change the design in multiple ways due to my interpretation of the instructions (which I found unclear and hard to follow).
- **`Task` grew a real notion of time and lifecycle.** The draft only had `duration_minutes` and boolean recurrence flags; the final version adds `due_date`, `scheduled_time`, and `is_complete`, plus a `next_occurrence()` method. I made this change because a recurring task needs to know *when* its next instance is due, and I needed a way to distinguish "done" from "not yet scheduled" — the draft's `is_scheduled` flag alone couldn't represent both concepts.
- **`Owner` gained a day window (`day_start`/`day_end`) and `get_tasks_by_pet_name()`.** 
- **`Scheduler` absorbed most of the new behavior**: `day_minutes()`, `sort_by_time()`, `sort_by_duration()`, `detect_conflicts()`, and the private helpers `_minutes_between` and `_in_window`. The draft's `Scheduler` only had `generate_plan`, `_fits_in_time`, and `_sort_tasks`. Conflict detection in particular wasn't part of the original design at all — it came out of asking "what happens if two pets both need attention at 8 AM?" while implementing, which the draft hadn't accounted for.
- **`Pet` picked up `complete_task()` and `filter_tasks_by_completion()`**

## 2. Scheduling Logic and Tradeoffs

**a. Constraints and priorities**

- What constraints does your scheduler consider (for example: time, priority, preferences)?
- How did you decide which constraints mattered most?

The scheduler considers four constraints: explicit clock time, priority, recurrence frequency, and task duration. Clock time is treated as the hardest constraint — tasks with a set `scheduled_time` are always placed first in the plan, in chronological order, because a vet appointment at 9 AM is a fixed commitment that cannot be moved. Within that group, and for all unscheduled tasks that follow, priority acts as the tiebreaker since it directly reflects the owner's stated importance. Recurrence frequency is considered next: a one-time task is assumed to be more special than a weekly or daily one, so it is preferred when priorities are equal. Duration is the softest constraint — task times are often approximate in practice, and shorter tasks can usually be squeezed in outside the formal schedule. The scheduler also performs conflict detection across all pets, warning when two tasks share the same `scheduled_time` so the owner can resolve overlaps without the program ever crashing or refusing to build a plan.

**b. Tradeoffs**

- Describe one tradeoff your scheduler makes.
- Why is that tradeoff reasonable for this scenario?

The scheduler greedily fits tasks into available time using the four-constraint ordering described above. The main tradeoff is that a single long, high-priority task with an early `scheduled_time` can consume most of the available time, causing several shorter lower-priority tasks to be skipped entirely — even if those tasks could have collectively fit in less time than the one that blocked them. This is a reasonable tradeoff because the scheduler is built around hard commitments first and owner-stated priorities second; automatically reordering or splitting tasks to maximize coverage would undermine the guarantee that fixed appointments and high-priority work always get scheduled. For scheduling conflicts — when two tasks across different pets share the same `scheduled_time` — the scheduler takes a lightweight approach and issues a warning message rather than blocking the plan, trusting the owner to resolve the overlap rather than producing an empty or incomplete schedule.

## 3. AI Collaboration

**a. How you used AI**

- How did you use AI tools during this project (for example: design brainstorming, debugging, refactoring)?
- What kinds of prompts or questions were most helpful?

I used AI to draft the initial UML from the scenario description, converting that UML into Python class stubs, filling in the scheduling logic (sorting, conflict detection, recurrence, plan generation), writing the pytest suite, and wiring `pawpal_system.py` into the Streamlit UI in `app.py`. The most useful prompts were narrow and behavior specific prompts, such as asking it to implement `detect_conflicts` given the existing `Task`/`Pet`/`Owner` shapes, or to write tests for a specific edge case rather than open-ended "build the scheduler" requests. Asking it to explain why it chose a particular sort key or tie-break order was also useful for catching design decisions I wouldn't have thought to ask about directly.

**b. Judgment and verification**

- Describe one moment where you did not accept an AI suggestion as-is.
- How did you evaluate or verify what the AI suggested?

When building `generate_plan`, the AI's first pass sorted tasks by priority only, ignoring `scheduled_time` entirely. I pushed back because a task pinned to a specific clock time (like a vet appointment) should behave like a hard commitment, not just another priority-ranked item (otherwise a low-priority but time-fixed task could get silently skipped in favor of a flexible high-priority one). I verified this using test cases that mixed timed and untimed tasks at different priorities and checking that the timed ones landed in the schedule in chronological order first, which is what led to the current `_sort_tasks` ordering (`scheduled_time` first, priority as tiebreaker). I also ran `main.py` directly and read the printed schedule by hand to confirm the ordering matched the day-window and budget constraints before trusting the test suite alone.

## 4. Testing and Verification

**a. What you tested**

- What behaviors did you test?
- Why were these tests important?

I tested six areas: sorting (chronological order, stable ties, priority order, shortest-duration-first), recurrence (daily/weekly next-occurrence generation, non-recurring tasks producing nothing, completing a recurring task exactly once appending exactly one new task), conflict detection (same time across two pets, same time on one pet, unscheduled tasks ignored, no false positives when there's no overlap), plan generation (over-budget tasks landing in `skipped_tasks`, scheduled tasks marked `is_scheduled = True`), completion filtering, and basic task/pet state mutation (`mark_complete`, `add_task`). These mattered most around the two places where a subtle bug would be easy to miss and hard to notice visually: recurrence (make sure completing a task doesn't create duplicate copies or leak state onto the original task) and conflict detection (make sure it doesn't flag unscheduled tasks against each other, since `None == None` would otherwise look like a false conflict).

**b. Confidence**

- How confident are you that your scheduler works correctly?
- What edge cases would you test next if you had more time?

My confidence is at 4/5 for the core scheduling behaviors — sorting, recurrence, and conflict detection are well covered and pass consistently. What I'd test next is the multi-day rollover behavior for recurring tasks (the current `next_occurrence` uses `date.today()`, so I'd want tests that don't depend on wall-clock time), a day window that spans midnight, and tasks with a `duration_minutes` longer than the entire day window.

## 5. Reflection

**a. What went well**

- What part of this project are you most satisfied with?

I'm most satisfied with the `generate_plan`/`_sort_tasks` logic and its test coverage. The ordering rules (hard clock-time commitments first, priority as tiebreaker, then a greedy fit against the remaining budget) map cleanly onto how an actual pet owner would think about their day, and the tests give me real confidence that edge cases like over-budget tasks or out-of-window times behave correctly instead of just "looking right" in a demo run.

**b. What you would improve**

- If you had another iteration, what would you improve or redesign?

If I had anothe iteration, I would make it so that the user can edit the schedule by dragging task "cards". currently if a user want to change the schedule they have to go to the task sections and change numbers, which is less convenient.

**c. Key takeaway**

- What is one important thing you learned about designing systems or working with AI on this project?

The most important thing I learned is that AI is much more useful when I supply the constraints up front than when I ask it to infer them. The scheduler only ended up with the right behavior (clock time as a hard constraint, priority as a soft tiebreaker) because I specified that ordering explicitly and then tested against it — when I left it open-ended, the AI defaulted to a plausible but wrong prioritization. Treating AI output as a first draft to verify against concrete test cases, rather than a finished answer, was the difference between a scheduler that looked right and one I could actually trust.
