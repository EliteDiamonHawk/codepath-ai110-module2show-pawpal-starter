## 2. Scheduling Logic and Tradeoffs

**a. Constraints and priorities**

- What constraints does your scheduler consider (for example: time, priority, preferences)?
- How did you decide which constraints mattered most?

The scheduler considers four constraints: explicit clock time, priority, recurrence frequency, and task duration. Clock time is treated as the hardest constraint — tasks with a set `scheduled_time` are always placed first in the plan, in chronological order, because a vet appointment at 9 AM is a fixed commitment that cannot be moved. Within that group, and for all unscheduled tasks that follow, priority acts as the tiebreaker since it directly reflects the owner's stated importance. Recurrence frequency is considered next: a one-time task is assumed to be more special than a weekly or daily one, so it is preferred when priorities are equal. Duration is the softest constraint — task times are often approximate in practice, and shorter tasks can usually be squeezed in outside the formal schedule. The scheduler also performs conflict detection across all pets, warning when two tasks share the same `scheduled_time` so the owner can resolve overlaps without the program ever crashing or refusing to build a plan.

**b. Tradeoffs**

- Describe one tradeoff your scheduler makes.
- Why is that tradeoff reasonable for this scenario?

The scheduler greedily fits tasks into available time using the four-constraint ordering described above. The main tradeoff is that a single long, high-priority task with an early `scheduled_time` can consume most of the available time, causing several shorter lower-priority tasks to be skipped entirely — even if those tasks could have collectively fit in less time than the one that blocked them. This is a reasonable tradeoff because the scheduler is built around hard commitments first and owner-stated priorities second; automatically reordering or splitting tasks to maximize coverage would undermine the guarantee that fixed appointments and high-priority work always get scheduled. For scheduling conflicts — when two tasks across different pets share the same `scheduled_time` — the scheduler takes a lightweight approach and issues a warning message rather than blocking the plan, trusting the owner to resolve the overlap rather than producing an empty or incomplete schedule.
